Because ShipReq is unlike anything you've ever used before.

Some of the many aspects that distinguish ShipReq from seemingly similar products are:
* innovatively designed from the ground up from first principals -- this isn't just "a better JIRA/Word/Excel/whatever".
  Literally many, many months of nothing but analysis of the problems it aims to solve, and innovation of completely new approaches
* extremely fast -- nearly everything happens in your browser without having to go through the internet and talk to our servers on every interaction.
  In fact it's 98% capable of working completely offline (with that final 2% scheduled to be implemented in the future).
* extremely high-quality -- it's received a lot of attention to detail, very careful consideration and validation of edge cases, bugs are very rare, it's a core value that ShipReq will never become flakey, annoying, or unreliable.
* "we eat our own dog food" -- we at ShipReq use ShipReq to develop ShipReq. We're user #1 and we get to ensure a good experience without other users having to be  guinea pigs.
* highly configurable and flexible -- each feature receives a lot of very careful consideration and analysis to remove any implicit assumptions and constraints.
  Teams and people operate differently and it's very important that you have the freedom to work exactly how you need.
* it's similar to a compiler but for your requirements and issues instead of code -
  like compiler errors, we prevent you from doing most things that would break data integrity.
  like compiler warnings, there are some things that we allow (because of ambiguity, or just to mirror real-life change), but will present as issues for a human to resolve
  like a reliable IDE, you can rename and move data around, and be assured that all other data is automatically updated to maintain data integrity.
* never lose data - data is never permanently discarded. Like git and other version control tools, you always have access to the entire history, and point-in-time.
  In fact there are many decision decisions in place to make it as easy as possible to make experimental changes, fail, and quickly get back to your original state; even if you're affecting hundreds of requirements at a time.
* aims to solve offline problems as well as data-based ones. Interactions happen socially, away from computers, and ShipReq aims both handle those scenarios, and aid people in those scenarios.
* real time -- Collaborators see each others changes in real-time. You can also open up multiple tabs just by yourself and each tab will be updated to changes made in the other tab, in real time.
* makes maintaining requirements, issues, etc as quick and easy as possible. Data goes out of date on real-world projects because it becomes tedious admin, and eventually just becomes box-ticking with nobody really trusting it to be accurate. Creative endeavours of any kind are hard work enough as is, and you shouldn't have to spend lots of time on admin/maintenance/etc for little value. One of ShipReq's core values is for you to spend as little time as possible in ShipReq, and get the maximum amount of value, not just for the little time you put it, but even compared to what you'd get putting lots of time into alternative tools.
* happiness -- we want to bring happiness to our users' lives. We want enthusiastic users to be ecstatic, we even want reluctant users to be happy too, at a minimum just by giving them what they need as quickly and effortlessly as possible, and then getting out of the way.


ShipReq also brings to life an important philosophy of mine (David Barri, the founder and creator).
There's a lot of *"rush rush rush PIVOT! PIVOT! rush rush sorry we actually need something completely different, ok cool rush rush rush"*
that goes on in the software development industry. There are contexts in which those attitudes make sense and are smart,
but I've personally been involved in a lot of the other contexts where those trade-offs aren't appropriate for the situation, are used anyway,
and end up being extraordinarily detrimental to everyone involved.
I strongly believe that there are many cases in which a different approach will lead to more efficiency (thus lower costs, especially over time),
and more team morale. I believe that if you put in extra time, care, and effort
and the beginning of a project to ensure that all of foundations are of high quality, then that enables you and your team to build
all future features, and make all future changes without the typical problems that large projects encounter when they get to a certain size;
things like quality degradation, fear of change due to fear of bugs, changes costing more money because they cost more time because devs and testers
have to navigate more complexity and fragility with each added change.
In the creation of ShipReq I was able to test my own beliefs and thankfully, it's worked out exactly as I'd hoped.
Users of ShipReq should feel that everything works really well (sometimes shockingly well), that it's really fast,
and should feel a subtle sense of joy in their interactions.
